Beyond Kimchi: 7 Fermented Foods You Should Actually Try in 2026

April 24, 2026 · 4 min read

The gut health revolution isn't slowing down. But if your fermented food repertoire stops at yogurt and kimchi, you're missing out on some seriously delicious options that are hitting mainstream shelves this year.

1. Tepache

This Mexican fermented pineapple drink is fizzy, slightly sweet, and subtly funky. It's been a street beverage in Mexico for centuries, but craft tepache brands are now popping up at Whole Foods and Trader Joe's. Think of it as kombucha's cooler, tropical cousin.

2. Shio Koji

Japanese rice koji fermented with salt creates a paste that's basically a flavor cheat code. Marinate chicken in it for 30 minutes and it tastes like you brined it overnight. Chefs have known about this for years — now home cooks are catching on.

3. Curtido

El Salvador's answer to sauerkraut. This lightly fermented cabbage relish with oregano and chili flakes is traditionally served with pupusas, but it works on tacos, sandwiches, or straight out of the jar. It ferments in just 1-3 days at room temperature.

4. Water Kefir

Unlike milk kefir, water kefir is dairy-free, lighter, and incredibly easy to flavor. Ferment sugar water with kefir grains, add fruit juice, and you've got a probiotic soda that puts La Croix to shame.

5. Dosa Batter

The fermented rice-and-lentil batter used for South Indian dosas and idlis is one of the most nutritious fermented foods on earth. The fermentation breaks down antinutrients, increases B vitamins, and creates that signature tangy flavor. Pre-made batter is now available at most Indian grocery stores.

6. Miso (Beyond Soup)

Everyone knows miso soup. But miso as a cooking ingredient is underused. Stir white miso into butter for an umami compound butter. Blend red miso into salad dressings. Glaze salmon with miso and maple syrup. It's a one-ingredient flavor upgrade for almost anything savory.

7. Injera Starter

The spongy, tangy Ethiopian flatbread injera is made from fermented teff flour. With teff becoming more available in mainstream stores, home cooks are experimenting with injera as a gluten-free, probiotic alternative to regular bread. The fermentation takes 2-3 days but the results are worth it.

The common thread: fermented foods aren't just good for your gut — they make everything taste better. Start with one new one this week.
