What Your City's Tap Water Actually Contains (And How to Check)

April 23, 2026 · 4 min read

Most Americans drink tap water without a second thought. But after high-profile contamination events from Flint to Jackson, Mississippi, more people are asking: what's actually in my water? The answer is more accessible than you think.

Your Right to Know

Every public water system in the U.S. is required to publish an annual Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) — also called a Water Quality Report. It lists every detected contaminant, the levels found, and the EPA's maximum allowable limits. Your utility must deliver this report by July 1 each year, and most publish it online.

How to Find Your Report

Go to the EPA's website and search by ZIP code, or simply Google "[your city] water quality report 2026." You can also call your water utility directly. The report will list regulated contaminants like lead, chlorine, nitrates, and various bacteria, along with unregulated chemicals that were detected.

The PFAS Problem

The biggest water quality story in 2026 is PFAS — "forever chemicals" found in everything from nonstick cookware to firefighting foam. The EPA finalized enforceable PFAS limits in 2024, and utilities are now scrambling to comply. Many city reports now include PFAS data for the first time. If yours doesn't, ask why.

Free Testing Options

If you're on well water (no CCR available) or want independent verification, many county health departments offer free or low-cost water testing. Home test kits from companies like Tap Score and SimpleLab cost $30-200 depending on what you're testing for and provide lab-grade results.

Filtration: What Actually Works

Not all filters are equal. Standard Brita pitchers handle chlorine taste and some heavy metals but don't touch PFAS. For comprehensive filtration, look for NSF 53 (lead) and NSF P473 (PFAS) certified filters. Reverse osmosis systems remove virtually everything but cost more and waste water. Activated carbon block filters (like Berkey or Clearly Filtered) hit a good middle ground.

Knowledge is the first step. Look up your city's water report this week — it takes five minutes, and you might be pleasantly surprised. Or you might decide it's time for a filter.
